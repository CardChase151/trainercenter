# What to Look for in a Card Vendor (and What to Avoid)

Whether you are buying from a local shop, a vendor at a convention, or someone online, not every seller has your best interest in mind. Here is how to tell the good ones from the ones to walk away from.

## Green Flags

**They let you look without pressure.** A good vendor is happy to let you browse, ask questions, and think about it. They are not hovering over you trying to close a sale.

**They explain their pricing.** If you ask why a card is priced a certain way, they can tell you. They reference recent sales, condition, and market trends. They are not making numbers up.

**They are honest about condition.** A good vendor will point out flaws in a card before you buy it. Whitening on the edges, a slight crease, off-center printing. If they are upfront about imperfections, you can trust them.

**They buy and sell at fair margins.** Every shop needs to make money, but there is a difference between a reasonable margin and lowballing you. If a card is worth $100 and they offer you $10, that is not a fair deal. A reasonable buy price is typically 50 to 70 percent of market value depending on demand and condition.

**They are part of the community.** They host events, they know their customers, and they care about the hobby beyond just making money.

## Red Flags

**They pressure you to sell your graded cards.** This is a big one. If someone at a show or a shop is aggressively trying to get you to sell your slabs, be careful. They likely know the value better than you do and they are trying to buy low.

**They price above market without justification.** Every seller can price however they want, but if their prices are consistently 30 to 50 percent above TCGPlayer or eBay market prices with no explanation, shop elsewhere.

**They get defensive when you check prices.** You should always be able to pull out your phone and look up a card. If a vendor gets annoyed or tells you their pricing is firm and non-negotiable while being well above market, that is a bad sign.

**They sell cards in sealed sleeves that you cannot inspect.** If you are spending real money on a raw card, you should be able to see it up close and check the condition. If they will not let you look, do not buy.

**They have no return policy and no reputation.** Online sellers with zero feedback, no social media presence, and no return policy are risky. Established shops and vendors stand behind what they sell.

## The Bottom Line

The best vendors are the ones who want you to come back. They price fairly, they are transparent, and they treat the hobby with respect. If something feels off, trust your gut and walk away. There are plenty of good sellers out there.
